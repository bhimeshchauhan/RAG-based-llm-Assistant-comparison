# Navigating Grief and Loss During the Caregiving Journey

**Author:** Rev. James Walker, MDiv, BCC  
**Published:** 2025-11-08  
**URL:** https://kindred.app/resources/grief-loss-caregiving

---

## Introduction

Grief doesn't wait until the end. For most caregivers, it begins much earlier—sometimes at the moment of diagnosis, sometimes gradually as abilities decline, sometimes in waves that come without warning.

Many caregivers feel guilty about grieving someone who is still alive. Others are confused by the complexity of their emotions: loving someone and also wishing the caregiving would end, feeling relief and sorrow simultaneously, mourning a relationship that has transformed beyond recognition.

This guide explores the many faces of grief in caregiving and offers gentle guidance for navigating this territory. The goal isn't to resolve grief—that's not how grief works. It's to help you understand your experience and find ways to carry it without being crushed.

---

## Understanding Anticipatory Grief

Anticipatory grief is the grief we experience before a death occurs. It's common when caring for someone with a progressive illness, though it can arise whenever change and loss are present.

### What You Might Be Grieving

- **Loss of your loved one's abilities.** Watching someone sharp and quick become confused. Watching someone active become dependent.
- **Loss of your relationship as it was.** The conversations you used to have. The activities you shared. The reciprocity that has become one-sided.
- **Loss of the future you planned.** Retirement travels that won't happen. Grandchildren who won't be known. Years of companionship cut short.
- **Loss of your own life.** Your freedom, career, social connections, health—all affected by caregiving.

### The Confusion of Contradictory Feelings

Anticipatory grief mixes with other emotions in disorienting ways:

- You may deeply wish your loved one's suffering would end while dreading their death.
- You may feel guilty for thinking about life after they're gone while they're still here.
- You may resent the demands of caregiving while knowing you'll miss this closeness when it's over.

All of these can coexist. They don't cancel each other out or make you a bad person. They make you human, grappling with an impossible situation.

## Disenfranchised Grief

Disenfranchised grief is grief that isn't openly acknowledged or socially supported. Caregivers experience this often.

### Common Experiences

**Grieving someone still alive.** Others may not recognize this as "real" grief. Comments like "At least she's still here" or "Be grateful for the time you have" can feel dismissive of genuine losses you're experiencing daily.

**Grieving invisible losses.** Your freedom, career, social life, health—these losses are real but often unseen by others who focus only on your loved one's situation.

**Expected to feel relief.** After a death, others may assume you're relieved because caregiving is over, not understanding the complexity of grief following prolonged illness.

### Finding Witnesses

Grief that isn't witnessed and validated tends to fester. Seek out people who understand:
- Support groups for caregivers
- Therapists experienced with caregiver issues
- Communities like Kindred where others share these experiences

In the [community discussion on emotional support](https://kindred.app/community/thread-emotional-support), caregivers describe reaching out on hard days—sitting in parking lots crying, needing someone to hear them without offering solutions. Having your grief witnessed matters.

## Grief at Transitions

Caregiving involves many transitions, each potentially triggering fresh grief:

- A new diagnosis or worsening prognosis
- A hospitalization or health crisis
- Moving from home to a facility
- Entering hospice care
- The death itself

### Allowing Yourself to Grieve

Each transition represents a loss—of abilities, independence, possibility, hope. You don't need permission to grieve these. They are real.

Some caregivers find simple rituals helpful for marking transitions: a moment of silence, writing in a journal, lighting a candle, a meaningful conversation with their loved one if possible. Rituals give form to feelings that are hard to express.

### Your Loved One's Grief

Your loved one may also be grieving—their health, their independence, their life as it was. They may express it as anger, withdrawal, denial, or agitation rather than sadness. Meeting these responses with compassion rather than frustration helps both of you—though this is hard when you're grieving too.

---

## When This Is Especially Hard

Grief in caregiving is particularly challenging when:

- **The person you're grieving doesn't recognize you.** Dementia can erase the relationship while the person is still physically present.
- **You're grieving and also angry.** At the disease, at your loved one's past choices, at family who doesn't help, at the unfairness.
- **Others minimize your grief.** They don't understand, and their attempts at comfort feel hollow.
- **You have no time to grieve.** The demands of caregiving leave no space to process what you're losing.
- **The relationship was complicated.** Grief is harder when love was mixed with resentment, estrangement, or past hurt.

### If This Doesn't Work Right Now

On days when grief feels overwhelming:
- You don't have to process it all today
- Find small ways to let emotion move through you: cry, write, walk
- Accept that some days will be harder than others
- Be as gentle with yourself as you would be with someone you love

If grief consistently interferes with functioning—you can't get out of bed, can't care for your loved one, can't see any way forward—please reach out to a mental health professional. This isn't weakness; it's appropriate response to overwhelming circumstances.

## Grief After Death

When your loved one dies, grief shifts but doesn't end.

### The Mix of Emotions

You may feel relief alongside sorrow:
- Relief that their suffering is over
- Relief that your caregiving duties have ended
- Relief that you can finally rest

This relief doesn't mean you didn't love them. It means you're human, and caregiving was hard.

### Feeling Lost

For months or years, your days revolved around your loved one's needs. Without that structure, you may feel adrift—unsure who you are, uncertain what to do with your time, lost without the purpose caregiving provided.

This disorientation is normal. Rebuilding takes time.

### The Grief Process

Allow it to unfold without rushing. There is no timeline for "getting over" a significant loss. Waves may return unexpectedly—at anniversaries, holidays, ordinary moments that trigger memories.

Progress isn't linear. You may have good days followed by terrible ones. This doesn't mean you're regressing.

---

## Complicated Grief

Sometimes grief becomes prolonged, intense, and interferes with daily functioning beyond what is typical.

Signs that professional support may be needed:
- Grief remains overwhelming months after the death
- You're unable to function in daily life
- You're having thoughts of self-harm or wishing you had died
- You're unable to accept the death or feel stuck in anger or guilt

This isn't weakness—it's recognition that some grief requires more support than self-help and time alone can provide.

## Self-Care Through Grief

Caring for yourself while grieving is essential, though it may feel difficult.

### The Basics

- **Be patient with yourself.** Grief is exhausting—emotionally, physically, cognitively. You may feel foggy, forgetful, unable to concentrate. Lower expectations.
- **Maintain basic care:** eating, sleeping, some movement. When everything feels hard, focus on fundamentals.
- **Ask for help** with practical tasks if you're overwhelmed.

### Finding Expression

Express grief in ways that feel right:
- Talking with people who understand
- Writing (no one ever needs to read it)
- Art, music, or creative outlets
- Rituals and memorials
- Time in nature or spiritual practice

Find outlets that allow emotions to flow rather than staying trapped.

### Professional Support

Grief counseling or therapy provides a safe space to process complex emotions. Support groups connect you with others who understand.

Consider seeking help even if your grief doesn't feel "bad enough." Prevention is easier than repair.

---

## Reality Check: Healing Isn't Linear

Grief doesn't follow a predictable path. You won't move neatly through stages toward acceptance. You'll have weeks when you feel okay, then a song or a smell will level you.

Healing doesn't mean forgetting or "moving on." It means finding ways to carry your loss while continuing to live. The grief becomes part of you, but gradually it may take up less space.

Some days, grief will feel manageable. Other days, it will feel as fresh as the first moment. Both are part of the process.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Anticipatory grief is normal—you can grieve someone who is still alive
- Grief often includes contradictory feelings: love and resentment, relief and sorrow
- Disenfranchised grief (loss others don't recognize) is common for caregivers; seek people who understand
- Each transition in caregiving—diagnoses, hospitalizations, moves—can trigger fresh grief
- After death, expect a mix of emotions including relief; this doesn't diminish your love
- Grief isn't linear. Good days and bad days will alternate.
- Be patient with yourself; grief is exhausting
- Maintain basic self-care and seek professional support if grief feels overwhelming
- Healing doesn't mean forgetting—it means finding ways to carry loss while continuing to live
- You don't have to grieve alone. Connection helps.
