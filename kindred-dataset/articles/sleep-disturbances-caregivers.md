# Addressing Sleep Disturbances: A Guide for Exhausted Caregivers

**Author:** Dr. Emily Nakamura, PsyD, CBSM  
**Published:** 2025-10-15  
**URL:** https://kindred.app/resources/sleep-disturbances-caregivers

---

## Introduction

Sleep deprivation is epidemic among caregivers. You know you need rest, but nighttime caregiving duties, anxiety about your loved one, and the general weight of stress can make adequate sleep feel impossible.

Many caregivers describe operating in a fog—making mistakes, snapping at family, feeling emotionally fragile, struggling to function. One caregiver in the Kindred community wrote about forgetting her father's morning medication, backing into a pole in a parking lot, and crying at a dog food commercial—all in the same week.

This isn't a personal failing. It's what happens when someone is chronically sleep-deprived while trying to manage one of the most demanding roles a person can take on.

This guide addresses common causes of caregiver sleep problems and offers approaches that may help—with the honest acknowledgment that some disruptions can't be fully eliminated while caregiving continues. The goal isn't perfect sleep; it's better sleep, more often.

---

## Understanding Your Sleep Challenges

Caregiver sleep problems have multiple sources. Identifying yours helps target solutions.

### Nighttime Caregiving Duties

If your loved one needs repositioning, medication, toileting assistance, or supervision during the night, your sleep will be fragmented. This is structural, not something willpower can fix.

People with dementia often have disrupted sleep-wake cycles—they may be active when you need to rest. Conditions like Parkinson's, heart failure, or pain can cause nighttime waking that requires your help.

### Anxiety and Racing Thoughts

Many caregivers lie awake even when their loved one is sleeping. You might mentally run through tomorrow's tasks, worry about health changes, or process difficult emotions from the day.

The hypervigilance of caregiving—always listening for sounds, always anticipating needs—can make it hard to fully relax, even when nothing is happening.

### Physical Discomfort

Back pain from lifting, tension headaches, general physical exhaustion—caregiving is hard on your body, and that affects sleep.

### Environmental Factors

Monitoring devices. Sleeping in a recliner to stay near your loved one. Disruptions from their movements and sounds. Many caregivers don't realize how much their sleep environment has degraded.

### Your Own Health

Underlying sleep disorders like sleep apnea deserve attention regardless of caregiving. Medications, menopause, chronic conditions—these can compound caregiving-related problems.

## Creating Conditions for Better Sleep

You may not eliminate all disruptions, but you can optimize conditions for better sleep when you have the opportunity.

### The Basics

**Consistent schedule:** Try to go to bed and wake at similar times, even when nights are interrupted. Your circadian rhythm benefits from regularity.

**Sleep-friendly environment:** Dark, quiet, cool. Use blackout curtains, white noise machines, or earplugs as needed. Invest in comfortable bedding—you spend (should spend) a third of your life in bed.

**Limit stimulants:** Caffeine and nicotine, especially later in the day. Large meals close to bedtime. Alcohol may help you fall asleep initially but disrupts sleep quality later.

**Wind-down routine:** In the hour before bed, dim lights, avoid screens (blue light suppresses melatonin), engage in calming activities. This signals your body that sleep is approaching.

### Addressing Physical Discomfort

Proper body mechanics when lifting, regular stretching, treatment for injuries or chronic pain—these reduce physical barriers to sleep. If you're carrying tension from caregiving, gentle stretching before bed can help.

## Managing Nighttime Caregiving

If nighttime duties are fragmenting your sleep, look for ways to minimize disruptions while still meeting your loved one's needs.

### Evaluate What's Necessary

Are you waking at certain times out of habit or anxiety rather than actual need? Consult with healthcare providers about which nighttime interventions are truly required.

### Strategies That May Help

**Video monitors** let you check on your loved one without getting up. Knowing you'll actually hear if something happens can help your brain relax.

**Bed alarms** alert you to movement, so you don't have to wake repeatedly to check.

**Bedside commodes** reduce the full production of getting to the bathroom. Caregivers in the Kindred community often describe this as a game-changer—it felt like giving up at first, but the reduced effort at 2am was worth it. See [community discussion on sleep solutions](https://kindred.app/community/thread-sleep-solutions).

**Medication timing:** Some medications have diuretic effects or other properties that cause nighttime waking. One caregiver discovered her father was taking a medication with diuretic effect at dinner; moving it to morning reduced nighttime bathroom trips significantly. Talk with healthcare providers about timing.

**Limit evening fluids** while ensuring adequate hydration earlier in the day.

### Trade-Off: Room Sharing

Some caregivers share a room to monitor their loved one; others find separate rooms improve everyone's sleep.

**Considerations:**
- How often does your loved one actually need help at night?
- Can a monitor provide adequate awareness from another room?
- Is your sleep environment adequate where you currently are?
- Would your loved one be unsafe alone overnight?

Neither arrangement is wrong. Evaluate what's actually working.

### Nighttime Help

If possible, consider:
- A family member taking one or two nights per week
- Professional overnight care, even occasionally (often cheaper than daytime care)
- Rotating schedules if multiple caregivers are available

Even one guaranteed full night of sleep per week can significantly improve functioning.

## Managing Anxiety and Racing Thoughts

For many caregivers, the body is tired but the mind won't turn off.

### "Worry Time"

Create a set time earlier in the day—perhaps 15-20 minutes in late afternoon—to think through concerns and write down needed actions. When worries arise at night, remind yourself: "This has been addressed. It will keep until tomorrow."

### Keep a Notepad by Your Bed

When something pops into your mind, write it down and let it go. Externalizing the thought stops your brain from circling back to it.

### Relaxation Techniques

Deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, body scan meditations—practiced regularly, these reduce physiological arousal. They work best when practiced during calmer times, so they're accessible during acute stress.

### Avoid Clock-Watching

Seeing the hours pass increases frustration. Turn clocks away from view.

### If Anxiety or Depression Is Significant

Talk with your healthcare provider. Treatment for mental health conditions improves sleep, and improved sleep often helps mental health. Sometimes medication is appropriate. Cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is highly effective for chronic sleep problems.

## When This Is Especially Hard

Sleep is particularly challenging when:

- **Nighttime caregiving is frequent and unavoidable.** Some conditions simply require overnight help.
- **You're the only caregiver.** There's no one to take over.
- **Your loved one has dementia with severe sleep disturbance.** Their nights and days are reversed.
- **You have your own health conditions** affecting sleep.
- **You're working** and can't nap during the day. (See [Balancing Work and Caregiving](https://kindred.app/community/thread-work-caregiving-balance).)

### If This Doesn't Work Right Now

On nights when nothing helps:
- Lower expectations for the following day
- Nap if at all possible, even 20 minutes
- Accept impairment and adjust accordingly (don't make major decisions when severely sleep-deprived)
- Be gentle with yourself

If sleep deprivation is chronic and severe, this is a health emergency—for you. Functioning on three hours of fragmented sleep for weeks or months causes serious harm. You may need structural changes to your caregiving arrangement, not just better sleep strategies.

---

## When to Seek Professional Help

Sleep deprivation has serious health consequences. Consider professional help if:

- Sleep problems persist despite implementing strategies
- You experience symptoms like loud snoring, gasping, or stopped breathing (possible sleep apnea)
- Daytime sleepiness affects your ability to function safely
- You're having accidents or making dangerous errors
- You feel unable to continue

**CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia)** is highly effective and now often available virtually—helpful for caregivers with limited time.

Don't dismiss your sleep needs as less important than caregiving responsibilities. You cannot sustain caregiving without adequate rest.

---

## Reality Check: Some Sleep Disruption May Be Unavoidable

Honesty: If your loved one requires frequent nighttime care, there may be no solution that restores you to full, uninterrupted sleep while you're the primary overnight caregiver.

This doesn't mean strategies are useless—they can improve things at the margins, which matters. But it may also mean that the caregiving arrangement itself needs to change:

- Bringing in overnight help
- Rotating responsibilities with family
- Considering whether home care remains sustainable

Sleep deprivation severe enough to affect functioning isn't just uncomfortable—it's dangerous for you and potentially for your loved one. This is worth taking seriously.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Sleep deprivation is common among caregivers and has serious consequences for health and care quality
- Identify your specific sleep barriers: nighttime duties, anxiety, physical discomfort, environment
- Optimize what you can: consistent schedule, sleep-friendly environment, wind-down routine
- Use strategies like monitors, bedside commodes, and medication timing adjustments to minimize nighttime disruptions
- Manage racing thoughts with "worry time," notepads, and relaxation techniques
- Consider whether room-sharing arrangements are actually helping or hurting
- Even occasional full nights of sleep (through nighttime help) can significantly improve functioning
- If anxiety or depression affects sleep, consider professional support
- Seek help for persistent problems—CBT-I is effective and increasingly accessible
- If chronic severe sleep deprivation continues despite efforts, the caregiving arrangement may need structural change
- Be gentle with yourself. Sleep problems aren't a personal failing.
